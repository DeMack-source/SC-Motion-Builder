Appellate Manual

This section separates appellate work from the motion builder. Use it as a checklist, timeline, and court-reference hub for notices of appeal, record designation, briefing, rehearing, and mandate preservation.

The appellate process is not one filing. It is a sequence of gates. Each gate answers a different question: is the appeal timely, is the record complete, is the legal issue preserved, and is there anything left to do after the opinion. The goal here is to make the path visible so a user and family member can understand what happens next, why it matters, and what documents usually unlock the next step.

How the Appellate Labyrinth Works
START
What judgment or order is being challenged?
Find the entered order, the docket date, and the deadline. Appellate time usually starts with entry, not with the hearing.
↓
BRANCH 1
Build the record first
Orders, transcripts, exhibits, motions, verdict forms, and sentencing materials are what the appellate court actually reads.
↓
BRANCH 2
Translate the record into a brief
State the issue, standard of review, record citation, law, and requested relief. The brief should match the record, not replace it.
↓
BRANCH 3
Watch the rehearing and mandate window
After the opinion, short deadlines may open for rehearing, clarification, certification, or a stay of mandate.
↓
NEXT QUESTION
Does the file continue after the opinion?
If the appeal is over, the next layer may be a postconviction motion, a further state filing, or eventually a federal claim after exhaustion.
Where Post-Conviction Motions Fit
The Florida path usually branches after direct appeal. Rule 3.850 and Rule 3.800 are state collateral tools that can correct different kinds of problems in the state judgment. If the state courts are given a fair chance to fix a federal constitutional error and the issue remains unresolved, the next layer may be federal habeas under 28 U.S.C. § 2254.
⚖️
Rule 3.850
Collateral postconviction relief
Use for claims like ineffective assistance, newly discovered evidence, involuntary plea, Brady issues, and some constitutional sentencing problems after the direct appeal path.
This is the motion most closely tied to later federal exhaustion because it gives the state courts a full chance to rule on the federal issue.
📋
Rule 3.800
Illegal sentence correction
Use for sentences that are illegal on their face, exceed the statutory maximum, or otherwise fit the narrow rule. Some sentencing issues are preserved here even while direct appeal is pending or after it ends.
This tool is different from 3.850. The goal is to correct a sentence problem, not relitigate the whole case.
🏛
Federal 2254
Federal collateral review
Use after state remedies are exhausted or fairly presented. The federal court reviews custody through a constitutional lens, not as a new trial court for the state case.
If the state courts never got the first full chance, the federal petition may not be ready yet.
📝
Direct Appeal
Fla. R. App. P. 9.110 / 9.140
Start with the notice deadline, the issue list, and the final order or judgment you are challenging. This is the entry point to the appellate maze: if the appeal is not timely, the later steps may never matter.
📚
Record & Brief Prep
Fla. R. App. P. 9.200 / 9.210
Track the record designation, transcript orders, brief deadlines, and the outline of the initial brief. This section helps the user understand that the brief is only as good as the record underneath it.
⏳
Rehearing / Mandate
Fla. R. App. P. 9.330 / 9.340 / 9.350
Preserve the post-decision window, monitor rehearing deadlines, and track when the mandate will issue. This is the short final corridor after the opinion, where timing can preserve or close the next level of review.
Appellate Timeline
1. Final judgment or order enters.
2. Notice of appeal deadline runs.
3. Designate the record and order transcripts.
4. Draft the brief from the record, not from memory.
5. If the opinion is unfavorable, track rehearing and mandate dates immediately.
This timeline is intentionally simplified. Real appellate practice may involve extensions, partial records, cross-appeals, post-judgment motions, or remand instructions from the appellate court. The manual should help a user understand the sequence without pretending every case follows the same script.
Florida Review Timeline
1. Judgment or appealable order is entered.
2. Notice of appeal deadline runs and the direct appeal begins if filed timely.
3. Record designation, transcript requests, and issue framing shape the appellate file.
4. Briefing and oral argument, if any, present the preserved issues to the appellate court.
5. Rehearing, clarification, certification, or mandate protection may follow the opinion.
6. If the judgment still stands, state collateral review may continue through Rule 3.850 or Rule 3.800 depending on the problem.
7. Once Florida courts have had the fair chance to resolve the federal claim, a § 2254 petition may be considered.
This is the sequence, not the strategy. Some issues live only in the direct appeal record, some require collateral fact development, and some must be handled as sentence corrections. The manual should show the path without collapsing every remedy into one bucket.
Common Exhaustion Problems
A federal court usually wants the claim to have been fairly presented to the Florida courts before it will review the issue. Common problems include:

1. Raising the wrong legal theory in state court and a different one in federal court.
2. Skipping a needed state filing, such as a Rule 3.850 motion for a fact-heavy claim.
3. Failing to give the Florida appellate court a fair chance to review the issue.
4. Waiting too long and running into deadlines, waiver, or procedural default.

The practical rule is simple: if the issue was not properly presented in the state process, the federal court may treat it as unexhausted or defaulted.
Do Not Skip The Gates
The appellate and postconviction path is not one straight road. Rehearing is not the same as a new appeal. Rule 3.850 is not the same as Rule 3.800. Federal habeas is not the same as either state remedy. Each gate has its own deadline and its own purpose.

If the case is still in direct review, focus on the appellate record and briefing. If the claim is collateral, determine whether it belongs in 3.850 or 3.800. If the federal claim has not been fairly presented to Florida, the federal court may not be ready for it yet.

Florida Legal Reference Library
⚖️
Rule 3.850 — Postconviction Relief
Fla. R. Crim. P. 3.850
Challenge convictions based on: ineffective assistance of counsel, newly discovered evidence, unconstitutional plea, or illegal sentence. Must be filed within 2 years of conviction becoming final.
⏱ The 2-year clock starts when direct appeal is exhausted. Newly discovered evidence may extend the deadline.
📋
Rule 3.800 — Illegal Sentence
Fla. R. Crim. P. 3.800(a)
Correct an illegal sentence at any time — no deadline. Sentence must exceed statutory maximum, fall below mandatory minimum, or violate constitutional protections.
🔑 No deadline. Can be filed even decades after sentencing if the sentence is truly illegal.
📝
Direct Appeal Process
Fla. R. App. P. 9.110 / 9.140
The appeal starts fast: preserve the deadline, identify reviewable issues, order the record, and brief the errors with record citations.
⏱ Confirm the notice-of-appeal deadline immediately after the judgment or order is entered. Missing it can forfeit review.
📚
Record Designation & Briefing
Fla. R. App. P. 9.200 / 9.210
The record on appeal and the brief work together. Designate the right transcripts, preserve the right exhibits, and write the brief to match the record rather than the other way around.
🧭 Build the brief outline from the record first, then fill in the legal authorities and citations.
⏳
Rehearing & Mandate
Fla. R. App. P. 9.330 / 9.340 / 9.350
After the opinion, the next deadlines matter. Rehearing, clarification, certification, and stay-of-mandate requests all have separate timing rules that can preserve further review.
⏱ Mark the rehearing deadline the day the opinion is released. Once the mandate issues, options narrow quickly.
🔒
Seal / Expunge — Florida
F.S. § 943.0585 / § 943.059
Expungement: destroys records (for charges not resulting in conviction). Sealing: hides from public but record still exists. One opportunity per lifetime. FDLE application required.
✓ After sealing, you may legally deny the arrest on most job applications.
🗓
Early Termination of Probation
Fla. Stat. § 948.04
After completing 50% of probation AND all special conditions, file a petition for early termination. Not a favor — a statutory right. P.O. "no objection" letter dramatically helps approval.
📋 Build an Evidence Box first: payment receipts, employment records, community service, certificates.
💰
Restitution Modification
Fla. Stat. § 775.089
Courts must consider financial resources and dependent obligations. Bearden v. Georgia (1983): you cannot be jailed solely for inability to pay. Document bona fide efforts.
📄 Attach Appendix B budget worksheet as Exhibit B to any modification motion.
🗳
Voting Rights — Amendment 4
Art. VI, § 4, Fla. Const.
Automatic restoration upon completion of ALL terms including LFOs. Not murder or felony sex offenses. Get Safe Harbor Advisory Opinion BEFORE registering. $1 owed = still ineligible.
📞 FRRC: 407-901-3749 — helps clear LFOs blocking voting rights.
🏛
Restoration of Civil Rights
Florida Rules of Executive Clemency
5–7 year wait after end of supervision. Full RCR restores jury service, public office eligibility, professional licensing. Build Clemency Binder now — it's what the Governor reads.
📞 FCOR: 850-488-2952 | FL Justice Center: 954-758-7555
💼
Employment — No Auto-Denial
Fla. Stat. § 112.011
Most Florida employers and licensing boards CANNOT automatically reject you solely for a felony. The offense must be directly related to the position. Use this to challenge blanket denials.
📝 Request written denial reason. If they cite only the conviction — that's challengeable.

Federal Habeas Path

Federal review under 28 U.S.C. § 2254 is not a shortcut around the state process. It is the next layer after state remedies are exhausted. In plain terms: the user usually moves from trial to direct appeal, then into Rule 3.850 or Rule 3.800 collateral review as needed, and only then into federal court if a federal constitutional claim remains uncorrected.

A federal habeas petition usually asks one question: did the state process produce a custody result that violates the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States? That is why the state record, the state appellate history, and the state collateral motions matter so much.

Florida Path Map
Direct appeal reviews the judgment or order already entered. It is the first review layer and usually focuses on errors preserved in the record.

Rule 3.850 is the main Florida postconviction motion for collateral claims like ineffective assistance, newly discovered evidence, involuntary plea issues, and other constitutional problems that depend on facts outside the direct-appeal record.

Rule 3.800 is the narrower sentence-correction path. It is used when the sentence itself is illegal or otherwise wrong under the rule, and it often works alongside the rest of the state case rather than replacing it.

Section 2254 comes after the Florida courts have had the first real chance to resolve the federal constitutional claim. If a claim was not fairly presented in state court, the federal filing may be premature or limited.
State to Federal Ladder
Step 1: Use Florida appeals and collateral motions to build the record
Florida direct appeal, Rule 3.850, and Rule 3.800 proceedings are where most of the important factual and legal issues are first preserved. Federal court usually will not rescue claims that were never fairly presented to the state courts. That means the state case history is part of the federal case history.

What matters here: the issues raised, how they were framed, what relief was requested, and what the state court actually decided.
Step 2: Exhaustion means the state courts had a fair chance to rule
Exhaustion is the idea that the state system should get the first full chance to correct the alleged federal constitutional error. For a Florida user, that usually means the claim has been presented through the Florida trial court and, when required, through the Florida appellate process. If a claim was never fairly presented, the federal court may dismiss or limit review.

Common failure point: trying to go federal before the state issue was actually given to the state courts in a way they could rule on.
Step 3: Section 2254 is a federal challenge to state custody
A 2254 petition is the federal habeas vehicle for a person in state custody who says the state-court judgment violates the Constitution or federal law. The federal petition should identify the state judgment being challenged, the constitutional grounds, the supporting facts, and the relief requested.

Common failure point: turning the federal petition into a general grievance rather than a specific constitutional challenge to one state judgment.
Step 4: File in the correct Florida federal district
Florida is split into three federal district courts: Northern, Middle, and Southern. The county of conviction usually points you to the district and division that handles the case. This matters because the court location, clerk's office, and filing instructions are district-specific.

Use the county router below to find the federal district attached to the Florida county involved in the state case.

Decision Tree: What To File Next
Start with posture, not preference. Follow the branch that matches where the case is right now.
START
What is the case posture right now?
Check the entered order, the notice deadline, the issue type, and whether Florida has already had a fair chance to rule.
↓
BRANCH 1
If the notice deadline is still open
Use the direct appeal. That is the first Florida review path and the one that preserves the record-based issues.
↓
BRANCH 2
If the problem depends on facts outside the trial record
Look first to Rule 3.850 when the claim needs collateral fact development, such as ineffective assistance or a plea issue.
↓
BRANCH 3
If the sentence is illegal on its face
Use Rule 3.800 for the narrow sentence-correction path.
↓
STOP / NEXT LAYER
If Florida already had a fair chance to rule on the federal claim
If the claim was fairly presented and is still unresolved, federal habeas under § 2254 may be the next layer.
The wrong filing can waste time, miss deadlines, or create exhaustion problems. When the branch is unclear, identify the order, the deadline, the issue type, and the last state court that actually saw the claim.
